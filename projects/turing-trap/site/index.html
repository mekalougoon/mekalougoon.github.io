The Industrial Revoloution

In the ticking of the clock, we find both progress and madness — each day a page in the story of machines and men.

What story will you have today?

Person #1

Today, I started at the mill, my first real job since Father’s accident. The machines are loud, so loud they drown out every thought in my head. My hands ache from feeding cotton through the spinning frames, and I barely had time to eat. But we need the coin, and with so many people coming to the city, there’s no rest for anyone.

Person #2

y first day at the mill, and the iron beast swallowed me whole. I thought I could be its master, thought I’d keep my mind free. But the noise—it devours thought, churns flesh into rhythm, heartbeat to metal. My hands ache, my mind feels hollowed. But we need this, don’t we? To survive?

Person #3

My first day on the job at the mill, and wow, it’s loud! The whole place is one big roar, and my hands are already sore. But I keep my head up because this is a start. I’m finally earning something, bringing something home. Who knows, maybe one day this work will take me somewhere.

Person #1

We’ve been hearing rumors of new inventions—machines that could take over our work completely. Mr. Cartwright’s loom is all anyone talks about. Part of me wonders if that means more work or no work at all. It’s unsettling, but for now, we keep on with our hands and feet, pushing these looms all day long.

Person #2

They talk of new machines, sleek and relentless, like something out of a nightmare. These things, they say, they don’t need us at all. Replacements. It’s strange—the thought, it twists in my head, like gears grinding. A part of me wants it gone, a part of me wants to fight. But mostly, I feel small.

Person #3

They say there’s a new machine coming—one that’ll do our jobs faster, maybe even replace some of us. People say it’s progress, but honestly, it makes me a little nervous. Still, I think, maybe this means something better is around the corner? Maybe there’s more out there for me, for all of us.

Person #1

Another worker lost his hand today. It happens more often now as the factories grow and the machines move faster. They say the Luddites are fighting back, smashing the machines at night. I understand their anger, but I can’t afford to lose this job, no matter how dangerous it is.

Person #2

Another man, crushed today—broken bone and blood on the factory floor. The Luddites—they’re out there, waiting, whispering rebellion into the night. I feel their anger, it flickers in me. But I bury it, hide it away. I don’t speak. This work…this work is my only tether, thin and frayed as it is. Still, a strange pull beckons.

Person #3

Today, an older worker told me stories about his life before the mill. He used to make things with his hands, beautiful things. It stuck with me, this idea of crafting something with pride. There’s talk of smashing the machines, of people fighting back, but I’m not sure that’s the way. Maybe we’re meant to build something better, not break it.

Person #1
The children at the mill look so small next to the machines. I feel guilty seeing my own son here, working beside me, but the wages don’t stretch far enough for us to survive otherwise. The factory owners barely notice us as they stroll through, looking for any signs of slowness. The long hours blur together, and it feels like life is just the hum of gears and the scrape of metal.
Person #2
I see children now, their small fingers lost in the machines’ jaws. My own brother, smaller than them, his laughter lost beneath the clank and hiss. It eats at me, the way these machines strip them down, piece by piece. Our lives reduced to parts, movements, hours lost in endless grind. We’re becoming iron, flesh and metal, all the same.
Person #3
They moved me to a quieter section today, working alongside a boy who seems to see a whole world beyond these walls. He asks questions about everything, and I catch myself dreaming with him. We talk about what we’d do if we didn’t have to spend all day here—imagine if we could learn, if we could use these machines as tools, not chains.

Person #1

The machines won’t stop their whispering, not even when the night comes. I hear them, in the walls, in the floor, clanking and turning, spinning webs in my head. They tell me I’m made of iron too, just like them, that my bones are gears and my blood is oil. I tried to pull away today, but my fingers—they wouldn’t let go. It’s all one machine now, and I’m part of it, aren’t I? Aren’t I?

Person #2

The machines are inside me now. I hear them, whispering my name, threading thoughts I never wanted. They say I belong, that I’m part of this hum, this endless dance of gears. I tried to pull away today, but my hands—they wouldn’t leave the loom. The machines, they sing in my bones. My bones, gears. My blood, oil.

Person #3

I met a woman today who talked of a school in the city. A place where people like me can learn, can grow. She said there are ways to escape this life if you’re willing to work for it. I tell myself to hold onto that hope, that maybe, just maybe, there’s a future out there waiting.
